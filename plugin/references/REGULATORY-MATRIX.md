## Data Protection Frameworks

Data protection laws affecting data center operators in Tier 1 and Tier 2 markets. Tier 1 countries have full detail; Tier 2 countries have summary entries.

### Tier 1 Countries

| Country | Law | Effective | Cross-Border Transfer | Breach Notification | DC-Specific Provisions | Confidence | Source |
|---------|-----|-----------|----------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|------------|--------|
| EU/EEA | GDPR (Regulation 2016/679) | May 25, 2018 | SCCs, BCRs, adequacy decisions | 72 hours to supervisory authority | Data processing agreements required for DC operators acting as processors; Art. 28 processor obligations | confirmed | [EUR-Lex](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2016/679/oj) |
| United Kingdom | UK GDPR + Data Protection Act 2018 | Jan 1, 2021 (post-Brexit) | UK International Data Transfer Agreement (IDTA), UK SCCs | 72 hours to ICO | Mirrors EU GDPR; UK adequacy decision from EU valid until June 2025, extended under review | confirmed | [ICO](https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/) |
| United States | No federal comprehensive law; sectoral (HIPAA, GLBA, CCPA/CPRA, state laws) | Varies by sector | No federal restriction; state laws vary (CCPA/CPRA for CA residents) | Varies by state (all 50 states have breach notification laws) | No federal DC-specific provisions; state-level incentive programs often include data handling requirements | confirmed | [NCSL Breach Notification Laws](https://www.ncsl.org/technology-and-communication/security-breach-notification-laws) |
| Canada | PIPEDA (federal) + provincial laws (Quebec Law 25, Alberta PIPA, BC PIPA) | Jan 1, 2001 (PIPEDA); Quebec Law 25 phased 2022-2024 | Accountability principle -- no restriction but controller remains liable | PIPEDA: "as soon as feasible"; Quebec: 72 hours | Quebec Law 25 requires privacy impact assessments for cross-border transfers; affects DC operator data flows | confirmed | [OPC](https://www.priv.gc.ca/en/privacy-topics/privacy-laws-in-canada/) |
| Australia | Privacy Act 1988 + Australian Privacy Principles (APPs) | Dec 22, 2001 (APPs: Mar 2014) | APP 8: reasonable steps to ensure overseas recipient compliance | Notifiable Data Breaches scheme: 30 days assessment, then notify | No DC-specific provisions; critical infrastructure reforms (SOCI Act 2018, amended 2022) apply to DC operators in designated sectors | confirmed | [OAIC](https://www.oaic.gov.au/privacy/the-privacy-act) |
| Japan | Act on Protection of Personal Information (APPI) | Apr 1, 2022 (amended) | Consent or equivalent protection in receiving country; PPC adequacy decisions (EU mutual adequacy) | Report to PPC "promptly" (guidelines: 3-5 days for initial, 30 days for full) | DC operators handling personal data must appoint a data protection manager; cross-border transfer rules tightened in 2022 amendments | confirmed | [PPC Japan](https://www.ppc.go.jp/en/) |
| Singapore | Personal Data Protection Act 2012 (PDPA), amended 2021 | Jul 2, 2014 (amendments: Feb 1, 2021) | Transfer must ensure comparable protection; PDPC recognized frameworks | Notify PDPC within 3 days of assessment (if significant) | Singapore encourages DC development; PDPA data protection trustmark scheme provides compliance certification | confirmed | [PDPC Singapore](https://www.pdpc.gov.sg/overview-of-pdpa/the-legislation/personal-data-protection-act) |
| India | Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 (DPDPA) | Aug 11, 2023 (rules pending, expected 2026) | Transfer allowed except to countries on government blacklist (list not yet published) | Notify Data Protection Board "without delay" (specific timeline in pending rules) | Implementation rules pending; DC operators should prepare for consent management and data fiduciary obligations | unconfirmed | [MeitY India](https://www.meity.gov.in/data-protection-framework) |
| South Korea | Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA), amended 2023 | Sep 15, 2023 (amendments) | Consent or equivalent protection; PIPC adequacy decisions (EU mutual adequacy) | 72 hours to PIPC and data subjects | DC operators processing personal information must designate a Chief Privacy Officer and conduct regular audits | confirmed | [PIPC Korea](https://www.pipc.go.kr/eng/index.do) |
| Brazil | Lei Geral de Protecao de Dados (LGPD) | Sep 18, 2020 | ANPD adequacy decisions, SCCs, BCRs (regulations in development) | "Reasonable time" to ANPD (specific timelines expected in pending regulation) | National Data Protection Authority (ANPD) still developing enforcement framework; DC operators must appoint a DPO equivalent (encarregado) | confirmed | [ANPD Brazil](https://www.gov.br/anpd/pt-br) |

## AI Regulation

AI regulatory frameworks affecting data center operators. Focus on provisions that impact DC infrastructure, compute requirements, and operational obligations.

| Jurisdiction | Law/Framework | Status | Key DC Impact | Effective Date | Confidence | Source |
|-------------|---------------|--------|---------------|---------------|------------|--------|
| EU | AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) | In force; full application Aug 2, 2026 | General-purpose AI model providers face compute reporting obligations; DC operators hosting AI training may need to track and report compute usage (FLOP thresholds). Digital Omnibus Directive (Nov 2025) extended some compliance deadlines to Dec 2027 for certain SME provisions. | Prohibited practices: Feb 2, 2025. Full application: Aug 2, 2026. Extended provisions: Dec 2027. | confirmed | [EUR-Lex AI Act](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32024R1689) |
| EU | AI Act -- Compute thresholds | In force | GPAI models trained with >10^25 FLOP classified as "systemic risk" -- requires compute tracking infrastructure at DC level. DC operators providing compute-as-a-service may face indirect obligations to facilitate provider compliance. | Aug 2, 2026 | confirmed | [AI Act Art. 51](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32024R1689) |
| United States (Federal) | No comprehensive federal AI law | No binding legislation | Executive Order 14110 (Oct 2023) on AI safety was rescinded Jan 2025 by EO 14179. No federal compute reporting obligations. Federal policy currently favors voluntary frameworks and sector-specific guidance. | N/A | confirmed | [White House AI EO 14179](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/removing-barriers-to-american-leadership-in-artificial-intelligence/) |
| United States (Colorado) | Colorado AI Act (SB 21-169, amended SB 24-205) | Effective Feb 1, 2026 | Requires "deployers" of high-risk AI systems to conduct impact assessments. DC operators are not direct targets but may face contractual obligations from AI deployer tenants. | Feb 1, 2026 | confirmed | [Colorado Legislature](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) |
| United States (Illinois) | Illinois AI Video Interview Act + proposed Artificial Intelligence Accountability Act | Video Interview Act active; Accountability Act pending | Limited DC impact; focus on employer use of AI. Proposed accountability act would require algorithmic impact assessments. | Video: Jan 1, 2020; Accountability: pending | unconfirmed (accountability act) | [Illinois Legislature](https://www.ilga.gov/) |
| United States (Texas) | Texas Responsible AI Governance Act (TRAIGA, HB 1709) | Effective Sep 1, 2025 | Requires AI system deployers to conduct impact assessments for high-risk systems. DC operators hosting AI training may face tenant compliance requirements. | Sep 1, 2025 | confirmed | [Texas Legislature](https://capitol.texas.gov/BillLookup/History.aspx?LegSess=89R&Bill=HB1709) |
| United Kingdom | Pro-Innovation AI Regulation (White Paper, updated 2024) | Non-binding framework; AI Safety Institute operational | No statutory compute reporting. AISI conducts voluntary pre-deployment testing. DC operators face no direct regulatory burden; UK positioning as AI-friendly jurisdiction. | Framework published Feb 2024 | confirmed | [UK DSIT](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/ai-regulation-a-pro-innovation-approach) |
| China | Interim Measures for Generative AI (effective Aug 15, 2023) + Algorithm Recommendation Provisions + Deep Synthesis Provisions | Active | AI service providers must register algorithms with CAC. DC operators hosting generative AI training must ensure content compliance. Compute providers may face licensing requirements for AI training workloads. | Multiple: 2022-2023 | confirmed | [CAC China](http://www.cac.gov.cn/) |
| Canada | Artificial Intelligence and Data Act (AIDA, Part 3 of Bill C-27) | Died on order paper (Parliament prorogued Jan 2025); future uncertain | Would have created AI commissioner and high-impact AI system requirements. No current federal AI legislation. Provincial initiatives (Quebec, Ontario) emerging but early stage. | N/A -- bill did not pass | confirmed | [Parliament Canada](https://www.parl.ca/legisinfo/en/bill/44-1/c-27) |
| Japan | AI Guidelines for Business (v1.0, Apr 2024) | Voluntary guidelines | Non-binding guidelines encouraging responsible AI development. No compute reporting or DC operator obligations. Japan pursuing innovation-friendly approach similar to UK. | Apr 2024 (voluntary) | confirmed | [METI Japan](https://www.meti.go.jp/) |
| South Korea | AI Basic Act (Framework Act on AI) | Enacted Jan 2025; effective Jan 2026 | Establishes AI Committee and high-impact AI classification system. DC operators may face compute infrastructure requirements for designated AI services. | Jan 2026 | confirmed | [Korean Law Information Center](https://www.law.go.kr/) |

## Data Residency Requirements

Countries requiring data to remain in-jurisdiction, affecting data center site selection and architecture decisions.

| Country | Scope | Sectors Affected | DC Operator Impact | Exceptions | Confidence | Source |
|---------|-------|------------------|--------------------|-----------|------------|--------|
| Russia | Federal Law No. 242-FZ (Data Localization) | All personal data of Russian citizens | Databases containing Russian citizens' personal data must be stored on servers physically located in Russia. DC operators must provide in-country infrastructure. | Cross-border transfer allowed for certain purposes (consent, contract) but primary storage must remain in Russia | confirmed | [Consultant.ru Law 242-FZ](http://www.consultant.ru/document/cons_doc_LAW_167049/) |
| China | Cybersecurity Law (2017) + Data Security Law (2021) + Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL, 2021) | Critical information infrastructure operators (CIIOs), all personal information, "important data" | CIIOs must store personal information and important data in China. Outbound transfers require CAC security assessment for data above volume thresholds (>1M persons' PI or >100K sensitive PI). DC operators serving CIIOs must provide domestic infrastructure. | Government-approved security assessments, SCCs, or certification can enable limited transfers | confirmed | [CAC Outbound Data Transfer Rules](http://www.cac.gov.cn/) |
| Vietnam | Cybersecurity Law (2018) + Decree 13/2023/ND-CP | Internet service providers, telecom, social media, gaming, e-commerce operating in Vietnam | Must store data locally and establish local offices. Vietnamese user data must have a domestic copy even if also stored abroad. DC operators must provide in-country capacity. | Decree 13 allows some cross-border transfers with impact assessment filing to Ministry of Public Security | confirmed | [Vietnam Cybersecurity Law](https://thuvienphapluat.vn/van-ban/Cong-nghe-thong-tin/Luat-An-ninh-mang-2018-351416.aspx) |
| Indonesia | Government Regulation 71/2019 (Electronic System Operation) + UU PDP No. 27/2022 | Public electronic system operators (mandatory); private operators (encouraged) | Public sector electronic systems must have local DC infrastructure. Private sector must ensure data accessibility from Indonesia. Strategic electronic systems require domestic data storage. | Private sector operators can store data abroad if accessibility and supervision are maintained | confirmed | [Kominfo GR 71/2019](https://www.kominfo.go.id/) |
| India | Sector-specific (RBI for financial data, CERT-In for incident data, DPDPA pending rules) | Financial services (RBI mandated), telecom, government | RBI mandated payment system data localization (Apr 2018). Telecom data must be stored domestically. DPDPA implementation rules may restrict transfers to blacklisted countries. | DPDPA allows transfers to all countries except those on a government blacklist (not yet published) | confirmed (RBI); unconfirmed (DPDPA rules) | [RBI Data Localization Circular](https://www.rbi.org.in/Scripts/NotificationUser.aspx?Id=11244) |
| Nigeria | NDPA 2023 + NITDA guidelines | All personal data processed in Nigeria | Data controllers must ensure adequate protection for cross-border transfers. NDPC may designate countries with adequate protection. Local DC infrastructure preferred for government and financial sector data. | Transfer with NDPC-approved safeguards (consent, contractual clauses, adequacy) | confirmed | [NDPC Nigeria](https://ndpc.gov.ng/) |
| Saudi Arabia | PDPL + Cloud Computing Regulatory Framework (CITC) | Government data, telecom, financial services | Government classified data must remain in Saudi Arabia. Cloud providers must offer in-Kingdom deployment options. CITC cloud framework requires local data centers for certain service levels. | Private sector data may be transferred with SDAIA approval to countries with adequate protection | confirmed | [CITC Cloud Framework](https://www.cst.gov.sa/) |
| Turkey | KVKK (Law No. 6698, Personal Data Protection Law) | All personal data | Cross-border transfers require explicit consent OR transfer to a country with adequate protection (Turkey's adequacy list) OR binding corporate rules approved by KVKK Board. In practice, local storage is common default. | Adequate country list, BCRs, or explicit consent | confirmed | [KVKK Turkey](https://www.kvkk.gov.tr/) |
| Kazakhstan | Law on Personal Data and Their Protection (2013, amended 2023) | Personal data of Kazakh citizens, government data | Government data must be processed in Kazakhstan. Personal data localization requirements for electronic government services. DC operators serving government must maintain domestic infrastructure. | Private sector cross-border transfers allowed with consent | confirmed | [Kazakhstan eGov](https://egov.kz/) |

Source: [European Commission Adequacy Decisions](https://commission.europa.eu/law/law-topic/data-protection/international-dimension-data-protection/adequacy-decisions_en) | Confidence: confirmed

## Foreign Ownership Restrictions

Countries restricting foreign ownership of data center infrastructure, telecommunications assets, or critical information infrastructure.

| Country | Restriction Type | Threshold | Sector | Waiver Process | Confidence | Source |
|---------|-----------------|-----------|--------|---------------|------------|--------|
| China | Foreign investment restricted/prohibited in value-added telecom services | 50% foreign equity cap for value-added telecom (including IDC/ISP licenses) | Telecom, Internet Data Center (IDC) | MIIT approval; foreign investors typically use VIE structures or JV with Chinese majority partner | confirmed | [MIIT Catalogue](http://www.miit.gov.cn/) |
| India | FDI allowed up to 100% in data centers (automatic route) since 2020 | No cap for DC; 100% FDI on automatic route | Data centers (liberalized); telecom infrastructure (49% automatic, up to 100% with government approval) | Automatic route for DC; government approval route for telecom above 49% | confirmed | [DPIIT FDI Policy](https://dpiit.gov.in/policies-rules-and-acts/fdi-policy) |
| Indonesia | Foreign ownership restrictions via Negative Investment List (Presidential Regulation 10/2021) | Varies by sector; telecom operators may require local partner | Telecom infrastructure, data centers (sector-dependent) | BKPM (Investment Coordinating Board) approval; positive investment list may allow higher foreign ownership for priority sectors | confirmed | [BKPM Indonesia](https://www.bkpm.go.id/) |
| Russia | Foreign ownership restrictions on critical information infrastructure | Strategic enterprises law limits foreign control of CII operators | Telecom, critical infrastructure | Government commission approval required for foreign acquisition of strategic enterprise stakes >25% | confirmed | [Russian Strategic Enterprises Law](http://www.consultant.ru/) |
| Saudi Arabia | Telecom and IT services require CITC licensing; foreign ownership allowed with conditions | No blanket cap but licensing requirements; Saudi partner often required for government contracts | Telecom, cloud services, DC operations | CITC license application; SAGIA (now MISA) foreign investment license; local partner arrangements common | confirmed | [MISA Saudi Arabia](https://www.misa.gov.sa/) |
| Vietnam | Foreign investment cap in telecom services | 49% for basic telecom services; 65% for value-added telecom with VN partner holding 51%+ of JV | Telecom, internet services, data center services classified as telecom | MIC (Ministry of Information and Communications) license; JV structure required | confirmed | [MIC Vietnam](https://mic.gov.vn/) |
| Thailand | Foreign Business Act (1999) restricts telecom and IT services | 49% foreign ownership cap for telecom and IT services under List 2/3 | Telecom, IT services | Foreign Business License from Department of Business Development; Board of Investment (BOI) promotion may allow exemptions | confirmed | [DBD Thailand](https://www.dbd.go.th/) |
